Week 2: Descriptive Statistics & Data Exploration

Summarising, visualising, and making sense of data before formal testing

Why Explore Data First?

Before running any hypothesis test, you must understand your data. Variable types (from Week 1) determine what statistics and visualisations are appropriate. This is called Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).

EDA goals: Make sense of data, explore relationships between variables, identify patterns, and detect outliers — all before formal statistical testing.

Summarising Categorical Variables

Bar Chart & Frequency Table

  • Always present numbers and percentages for each category
  • Report the total number of observations
  • Be concise — don't include too many categories

Body Condition Score Example

BCS is an ordinal categorical variable. Use:

✘ Histogram and mean (ordinal data can't have a meaningful average)
✔ Bar chart and frequency table (correct approach)

Summarising Continuous Variables

Histogram

Plot a histogram first, then assess the distribution shape before choosing summary statistics.

Normal / Symmetrical

Report mean and standard deviation

$$\bar{x} \pm SD$$ Mean describes central tendency; SD describes spread around the mean.

Skewed / Non-Normal

Report median with range, min, max, Q1, Q3, IQR

Median is more robust to outliers. IQR (Q3 − Q1) captures the middle 50% of data.

Measures of Central Tendency

Mean ($\bar{x}$)

The arithmetic average. Sensitive to extreme values.

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$$

Best for: normally distributed data

Median

The middle value when data are sorted. Robust to outliers.

Best for: skewed data, data with outliers

Example: Seizures in Epileptic Dogs

Owners reported the number of seizures observed in a group of epileptic dogs over the course of a month (ranged from 0 to 15).

Variable type: Discrete (countable, approximates continuous)

Summary: The number of seizures ranged from 0 to 15, with a median of 0.

Remember from Week 1: You cannot calculate a meaningful mean for ordinal data (like BCS). The "average" of two extremes hides clinical reality. Always check your variable type before choosing a summary statistic.

Symmetrical vs Skewed Distributions

Symmetrical (Normal)

  • Mean ≈ Median ≈ Mode
  • Bell-shaped curve
  • Data spread evenly around centre

Skewed

  • Mean pulled toward the tail
  • Right-skewed: mean > median
  • Left-skewed: mean < median

Connecting to Week 3

Preview: Next week, we'll use the summary statistics learned today as inputs for hypothesis testing. The choice of parametric vs non-parametric tests depends on your data distribution. If data is normal/symmetrical → parametric tests. If skewed → non-parametric (rank-based) tests.
