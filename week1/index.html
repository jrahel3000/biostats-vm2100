Week 1: Foundations of Biostatistics

Evidence-Based Veterinary Medicine, variable types, and the statistical mindset

Welcome to the World of Biostatistics

Every day in clinical practice, you'll face crucial questions: Is this treatment truly effective? How accurate is this diagnostic test? What's the best course of action for this patient?

The answers lie in statistical thinking — it's not just about numbers, it's about making sense of the evidence in front of you. When you interpret blood test results, evaluate treatment outcomes, or decide between treatment options, you're already thinking in a statistical manner, even if you don't realize it.

Key principle: Data doesn't lie. People do. Statistics is your defence against being misled by numbers.

What is Evidence-Based Veterinary Medicine?

EBVM = the use of best available scientific evidence, in conjunction with clinical expertise and consideration of owner & patient factors, to make the best clinical decisions for patients.

🔬 Diagnosis

Will this diagnostic test identify the disease? Will it identify subclinical disease?

💊 Treatment

Will treatment A be more effective than treatment B? Design treatment based on data.

📈 Prognosis

Forecast the likelihood of a disease status. Critical for early-stage intervention.

⚠️ Risk Assessment

Probability of death or physical damage for animals undergoing certain procedures.

Course Intended Learning Outcomes

  • Understand, explain and apply basic statistical concepts, ideas and techniques
  • Describe, summarise and interpret data
  • Identify the principles of quantitative research design and describe concepts such as bias, sampling and non-sampling error, sample size
  • Describe and apply commonly used data analysis techniques: descriptive statistics, confidence interval, hypothesis testing, ANOVA
  • Conduct a literature search, critically evaluate literature and apply scientific evidence to decision-making

Challenge Your Statistical Instincts

The Farm Size Puzzle

Cows from which type of dairy farms tend to have the largest mean litter size?

A. Small farms   B. Medium-size farms   C. Large farms   D. Hard to tell

Hint — Sample Size Matters: Smaller farms are more likely to show extreme variations — both high and low — than large, standardized groups. The "villain" here is statistical variability. Extreme results are more common in small samples.

The Parvovirus Test Puzzle

Assume the Canine Parvovirus infection rate is 1 in 5,000 dogs. If a dog is infected, the rapid test accuracy is 99%. If a dog is not infected, the false positive rate is 1%.

If a dog tests positive, what is the probability it is truly infected?

A. ~0.01%   B. ~99%   C. ~2%   D. ~50%

The Counter-Intuitive Truth: The answer is ~2%. Why? Because the disease is rare (low prevalence). The 1% false positives from the thousands of healthy dogs completely outnumber the single true positive case. Context (prevalence) is just as important as test accuracy.

Key Concepts: Population, Sample, Variable

Population

The complete collection of all individuals or units we want to study and make inferences about.

Example: All dairy cattle in Australia.

Sample

A subset of individuals or units collected from the population — we can't exhaust all individuals in a population.

Example: 20 sheep sampled from Wales.

Variable

Anything that varies between individuals, which you can measure directly or indirectly.

Example: Age, gender, litter size, body weight.

Observation

An individual described by a set of data. In a spreadsheet: rows = individuals, columns = variables.

Variable Types

Quantitative / Numerical

Continuous

Can take any value within a range. Measured on a scale with infinite precision.

Examples: Body weight, temperature, height.

Discrete

Values change in steps — countable, finite possibilities.

Examples: Litter size, number of seizures, parity.

Qualitative / Categorical

Nominal

Categories with no intrinsic order. Binary is a special case of nominal.

Examples: Breed, gender, ID, presence/absence of disease.

Ordinal

Categories with some intrinsic order, but differences between levels have no objective meaning.

Examples: Body Condition Score (1-9), size (small/medium/large).

The "Average" Trap: Never calculate the mean of an ordinal variable! If you average BCS 1 (emaciated) and BCS 9 (severely obese), you get BCS 5 ("ideal weight") — but both animals are suffering from extreme conditions. The average hides the clinical reality because the numbers are labels, not quantities.

Critical Rule: Numerical or Categorical?

Ask yourself:

  1. Does it take a range of numerical values (objective), or does it fall into categories?
  2. Does it make sense to summarise it using an average? (e.g., male:1, female:0 — average of 0.5 is meaningless)

The Research Process

Formulate research question → Design study & collect data → Statistical analysis → Evaluate generalisation → Reach conclusion

Key considerations in sampling: how many groups, what sample size, how to collect data, from where and when. If insufficient sample size, return to the experimental design phase and re-collect data.
